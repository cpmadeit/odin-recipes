French Toast

Description

French Toast is a dish made of slices bread soaked in beaten eggs and milk and then pan fried. When served as a sweet dish, milk, sugar, vanilla and/or cinnamon are commonly added before pan-frying. It is usually topped with any combination of sugar (often powdered), butter, fruit, or syrup. May also be served as a savory dish.

The earliest known reference to French toast is in the Apicius, a collection of Latin recipes dating to the 1st century CE, where it is described as simply aliter dulcia ("another sweet dish"). The recipe says to "Break [slice] fine white bread, crust removed, into rather large pieces which soak in milk [and beaten eggs] fry in oil, cover with honey and serve".

A 14th-century German recipe uses the name Arme Ritter ("poor knight"), a name also used in English and the Nordic languages. Also in the 14th-century, Taillevent presented a recipe for "tostées dorées".

Italian 15th-century culinary expert Martino da Como offers a recipe.

The usual French name is pain perdu, "lost bread", reflecting its use of stale or otherwise "lost" bread — which gave birth to the metaphoric term pain perdu for sunk costs. It may also be called pain doré, "golden bread", in Canada. There are fifteenth-century English recipes for pain perdu.

An Austrian and Bavarian term is pafese or pofese, from zuppa pavese, referring to Pavia, Italy. The word "soup" in the dish's name refers to bread soaked in a liquid, a sop. In Hungary, it is commonly called bundáskenyér (lit. "coated bread").

Ingredients

  • 1 (1-pound) loaf of challah bread, cut into 1-inch thick slices (10-12 slices)
  • 1 1/2 cups of milk, heavycream, or a combination of the two
  • 6 large eggs
  • 2 tbsp maple syurp, plus more for serving
  • 1 tbsp vanilla extract
  • 1/2 tsp ground cinnamon
  • 1/4 teaspon salt
  • 2-3 tbsp unsalted butter

Steps

  1. arrange a rack in the middle of the oven and heat to 300 degrees F

  2. fit a wire rack inside rimmed baking sheet - place the bread in a single layer on the rack

    bake, flipping halfway through, until dry to the touch on both sides, about 15 minutes total

    remove from oven and let cool 5 minutes - meanwhile, make the custard

  3. place the milk or cream, eggs, maple syrup, vanilla, and cinnamon in a quart-sized liquid measuring cup

    whisk until fully combined - no streaks of egg remianing

    pour into a 9x13 baking dish

  4. add as many slices of challah as can fit in a single layer

    soak, flipping once, until drenched, but not falling apart, 1-2 minutes per side

    return to wire rack, letting excess drip off onto baking sheet, and repeat with remaining challah slices

  5. melt 2 tbsp of the butter in a large nonstick skillet over medium heat

    when the foaming subsides and the butter is sizzling but not brown, add 4 soaked challah slices

    cook until the bottoms are golden-brown and crispy, 3 - 4 minutes

    flip and cook until the second side is browned, about 3 minutes more

    add remaining 1 tbsp butter to the pan in between batches, swirling as it melts to ensure it doesn't burn

    serve warm with more maple syrup

  6. storage:

    leftovers can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 4 days
